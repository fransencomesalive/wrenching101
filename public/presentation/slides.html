Section 01 — Bike Types

Road Bikes

Road bikes prioritize efficiency, low weight, sharp steering, and aerodynamic position.

Typical tire range: about 28 to 32 mm. Newer race bikes often clear up to 32 mm. The position is lower and longer, optimized for sustained speed on pavement.

Reference: S-Works Tarmac SL8. A pure race bike with 32 mm clearance and published geometry across sizes.

Section 01 — Bike Types

Gravel Bikes

Gravel bikes add tire volume, stability, clearance, and all-day control.

Typical tire range: about 38 to 50 mm. Gravel is not one category. Some are road bikes with bigger tires. Some are nearly drop-bar mountain bikes. The common thread is more air volume and more confidence when the surface gets rough.

Reference: Scott Addict Gravel. Aero cues, 45 mm clearance, 1x or 2x compatible.

Section 01 — Bike Types

Cyclocross Bikes

Race tools for short, violent laps: grass, mud, barriers, accelerations, remounts.

Not just gravel bikes with bad manners. CX geometry balances quick handling with enough clearance for mud and off-camber turns. UCI racing limits tires to 33 mm, which shapes the category heavily.

Reference: Stevens Super Prestige. Race-spec CX with geometry built for repeated efforts, not loaded exploration.

Section 02 — Frame Materials

Aluminum

Light, stiff, relatively affordable, and very good at being a bike.

  • Construction. Welded from shaped and butted tubes. Hydroforming creates complex shapes for stiffness, clearance, and design.
  • Ride character. Old harshness reputation came from oversized, stiff tube designs and narrow tires. Modern aluminum can ride much better, especially with larger tires.
  • Cost and weight. Lower cost than carbon. Often lighter than steel at a given price. A strong privateer race choice.
  • Trade. Harder to repair than steel. Fatigue life matters over a very long timeline.
Section 02 — Frame Materials

Steel

Durable, repairable, tunable, and still very much alive.

  • Construction. Modern steel is usually chromoly or higher-end air-hardened tubing. Welded, brazed, or lugged. Smaller-diameter tubes with thinner walls.
  • Ride character. The "springy" feel comes from tube design and frame construction, not a supernatural property inside the metal.
  • Cost and weight. Can be affordable or extremely custom. Usually heavier than carbon or aluminum for performance road use.
  • Best for. Custom fit, touring, all-road, commuting, and riders who value longevity. Easy to repair compared with carbon or aluminum.
Section 02 — Frame Materials

Carbon

Light, shapeable, tunable, expensive, and very easy to market badly.

  • Construction. Carbon fiber plies in resin. Layup direction matters: carbon is directional, stiff one way and more compliant another depending on fiber orientation.
  • Ride character. Two frames that look similar can ride very differently. Shape freedom lets engineers tune stiffness, comfort, aero, and weight in ways metal cannot match as easily.
  • Cost and weight. Best material for high-end race performance when designed well. Expensive tooling. Crash damage can be hard to inspect.
  • Trade. Repair is possible but specialized. Not immune to impact damage that looks minor from the outside.

Fit Basics

Section 03 — Fit Basics

A good position lets you breathe, steer, pedal, brake,
and repeat the ride tomorrow.

Fit is not just "comfort." Race fit is not just "lower." A good fit supports the ride someone is trying to do.

Section 03 — Fit Basics

What Good Position Means

If a rider looks like they are bracing against the bike, the bike is not supporting them.

  • Stable hips. No rocking or dropping side to side through the pedal stroke.
  • Quiet shoulders. Not hunched, not reaching, not carrying tension.
  • Soft elbows. A slight bend absorbs road input. Locked elbows transmit everything.
  • Hands that do not go numb. Numbness means too much weight or nerve compression.
  • Knees that track cleanly. In line with the foot, not caving in or flaring out.
  • Power that does not require a wrestling match. The pedal stroke should feel round, not punchy.
Section 03 — Fit Basics

Stack and reach are measured from the bottom bracket because that is where pedaling happens.

Saddle first.
Cockpit second.

Do not fix a saddle problem with a stem if the saddle is wrong first.

Section 03 — Fit Basics

Endurance Fit

More upright, more sustainable, less aerodynamic.

  • Taller stack means bar height without a tower of spacers
  • Supports breathing, visibility, and control
  • Better for long efforts and rough surfaces
  • Endurance frames often have more built-in stack for a given size
 

Race Fit

Lower, longer, faster when the rider can support it.

  • Demands more hip mobility and core endurance
  • Race frames often start lower, expecting the rider to tolerate more bar drop
  • A low position that collapses breathing is not fast in the useful sense
  • Right for riders who can actually hold it
Section 03 — Fit Basics

The bike should carry you across saddle, pedals, and hands.

Hands are for control,
not bodyweight storage.

Section 03 — Fit Basics

Stack Height

Stack is how high the frame lets the front end start. More stack usually means easier bar height.

  • Endurance frames tend to provide more stack for a given size.
  • Race frames often start lower, expecting the rider to tolerate more bar drop.
  • Spacers and stems can modify the final cockpit, but they cannot make every frame fit every rider without changing handling, aesthetics, or both.
  • In the geometry section we will look at exact stack numbers across road, gravel, and cyclocross.
Section 03 — Fit Basics

The Bike Leaves Clues

Hands, neck, knees, saddle, feet, back. Persistent pain belongs with a fitter or clinician.

  • Front knee pain. Saddle too low or too far forward.
  • Back of knee pain. Saddle too high.
  • Hand numbness. Too much hand pressure, saddle tilt, reach, posture, glove fit, or bar setup.
  • Saddle discomfort. Saddle shape, width, tilt, height, or a stability problem elsewhere.
  • Lower back pain. Reach, bar drop, hip mobility, saddle stability, or fatigue.

Section 04 — Drivetrain Types

Early bikes had fewer gears, fewer choices, and much more character-building.

Modern bikes have more range,
more precision, and more standards
to misremember.

Section 04 — Drivetrain Types

The 2x Road Era

Two rings up front. Ten, eleven, or twelve cogs in back. Small jumps. Big range. More moving parts.

  • Why 2x dominated road. Wide range with relatively tight jumps between gears. Tight cadence steps matter on pavement.
  • The front shift. A large chainring for speed, a small one for climbing. The crossover requires managing chain angle.
  • The complexity cost. Front shifting, cross-chaining decisions, trim or electronic compensation, more adjustment points.
  • Still dominant on road. 11-speed went mainstream in 2012 with Shimano Dura-Ace. 12-speed is now common across road groups.
Section 04 — Drivetrain Types

The 1x Shift

One ring. Wider cassette. Fewer decisions. The front derailleur left the chat.

  • Where it started. Took off in mountain biking, then gravel and cyclocross, because clutched derailleurs and narrow-wide chainrings improved chain retention.
  • Narrow-wide teeth. SRAM X-SYNC chainrings use alternating tooth profiles to better control the chain on rough or muddy terrain.
  • The upside. Simpler to think about. Less likely to drop a chain at the front. Great for surfaces where dropping a chain is catastrophic.
  • The trade. Bigger jumps between some gears. Sometimes less ideal cadence on sustained fast pavement.
Section 04 — Drivetrain Types

Electronic Shifting

Motors, batteries, buttons, and firmware. Very good at repeating itself.

  • Shimano Di2. Brought road electronic shifting into the mainstream. Road wiring with junction boxes and satellite shifters.
  • SRAM AXS. Popularized wireless shifting and app-based setup. No wires at all. Bluetooth pairing and firmware updates via app.
  • What it changes. Reduces cable contamination issues and improves shift consistency. Eliminates cable tension drift.
  • What it does not change. Still depends on a straight hanger, clean drivetrain, and compatible parts. Changes the troubleshooting tree, not the need to understand the bike.
Section 04 — Drivetrain Types

Range

How easy and how hard the gears get. The spread between your lowest and highest gear.

  • A wide-range 1x cassette gives plenty of climbing gear
  • A 2x drivetrain can offer similar total range
  • Wide-range cassettes often run 10-51 or 10-52 tooth
 

Jumps

How smooth the steps feel between gears. The cadence disruption moving cog to cog.

  • Wide-range cassettes have larger jumps between some cogs
  • 2x can offer tighter cadence steps with front shifting
  • For gravel and CX, chain retention often beats perfect cadence spacing
Section 04 — Drivetrain Types

Fewer front shifts.
More chain control.
Less drama.

A 1x setup removes the front shift, where many dropped chains happen. Narrow-wide teeth and clutch derailleurs help the chain stay engaged over bumps. The point is tradeoffs, not team sports.

Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

The chain is a wear part.

Dirt turns it into
expensive sandpaper.

A dirty chain accelerates wear across the chain, cassette, and chainrings. The worse the contamination, the faster the drivetrain becomes a group project with a receipt.

Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

Lube Basics

Traditional drip lubes. Three types, three tradeoffs. Colorado dryness makes the choice matter more.

  • Wet lube. Durable, messy, better in wet conditions. The exterior of the chain should be wiped clean because external lube attracts grit.
  • Dry lube. Cleaner, less durable, better in dry conditions. Very dry conditions can strip lighter lubes quickly.
  • All-condition lube. Compromise by design. Decent in most conditions, optimal in none.
  • The rule. Lube the inside of the chain, not the outside. Apply to rollers, let it penetrate, then wipe the exterior clean.
Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

Hot wax and drip wax aim to put lubricant inside the chain without leaving an oily exterior.

Wax works because it dries solid
and sheds contamination
instead of collecting it.

A properly waxed chain runs clean to the touch, resists contamination, and can extend drivetrain life when maintained.

Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

Cleaning A Traditional
Lube Drivetrain

  • Degrease. Use citrus degreaser or bike-specific degreaser. Apply to chain, cassette, jockey wheels, and chainrings.
  • Scrub. A chain scrubber or brush. Get into the cassette cogs and around the jockey wheels.
  • Rinse carefully. Without blasting water into bearings, hubs, or bottom bracket.
  • Dry completely. Let the chain dry before lubing. Water and lube do not mix well.
  • Lube and wipe. Apply lube to rollers. Let it penetrate. Wipe the exterior clean. The chain should be lubricated inside, not wearing a wet jacket.
Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

Watch for: black paste on the chain, noisy shifting, squeaking, stiff links, and chain stretch.

More lube is not
more maintenance.
It is usually more dirt.

Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

Maintaining A
Waxed Chain

Keep it clean. Top it up. Re-wax before it gets loud.

  • Initial cleaning. Factory grease must be fully removed before hot waxing or drip wax conversion. This step decides everything.
  • After wet rides. Wipe the chain dry. Dirt that is wiped off while wet is dirt that does not grind in.
  • Top up intervals. Silca recommends top-ups or re-waxing around 150 to 200 miles depending on conditions and product.
  • Re-waxing. With hot wax, wipe debris off the chain before re-immersing, assuming no oil contamination. No full strip needed if you stayed wax-clean.
Section 05 — Drivetrain Maintenance

Use a chain checker. Replace chains before they wear far enough to damage cassettes and chainrings.

A worn chain teaches
the cassette bad habits.
Then the cassette gets expensive.

Tire Talk

Section 06 — Tire Talk

Tires affect comfort, grip, rolling resistance, puncture resistance, cornering, and confidence.

Your tires are the first
suspension system on the bike.

Pressure is not a bravery contest.

Section 06 — Tire Talk

Folding Tire
Construction

Simple parts, complicated behavior. Bead, casing, rubber, tread, protection layer.

  • Folding bead. Usually aramid fiber instead of a rigid wire bead. Lighter, packable, and used on performance tires.
  • Casing. Gives the tire its structure. Casing density (TPI) affects how easily the tire flexes and deforms over terrain.
  • Rubber compound. Controls grip and wear. Softer compound grips better and wears faster. Harder compound lasts longer.
  • Protection layers. Reduce punctures but can add stiffness and rolling resistance. Choose based on surface and priority.
Section 06 — Tire Talk

TPI Without
The Bro Science

TPI means threads per inch. Higher TPI can feel faster and more supple. Less rubber can mean less protection.

  • What higher TPI does. Casing flexes more easily, reducing energy lost to deformation and improving ride feel.
  • What TPI does not tell you. Rubber thickness, puncture layers, compound, sidewall construction, and intended use all matter more than the number alone.
  • The rule. A delicate race tire is not automatically the right tire for chunky gravel or group rides with bad road luck.
  • Pick for purpose. Race tire for race day. More durable casing for training, rough pavement, and less predictable conditions.
Section 06 — Tire Talk

Tread Comes First

Choose tread for surface. Rolling resistance is useful, but the wrong tread on the wrong surface wastes more than watts.

  • Pavement. Slick or file tread. Low rolling resistance, minimal deformation on smooth surfaces.
  • Fine gravel. Low knobs with a fast center. Speed on hardpack with light edge traction.
  • Mixed gravel. Supported shoulder knobs. Center rolls fast, shoulders grip in loose or off-camber sections.
  • Chunky gravel. Volume and protection. Bigger air volume to absorb hits. More aggressive tread for grip.
  • Mud. Open tread that clears. Packed lugs become slick. Open lugs shed mud and find traction underneath.
Section 06 — Tire Talk

Width And
Rim Width

Tire width is not just the number printed on the sidewall. The rim changes the shape.

  • Internal rim width matters. Wider rims make a tire measure wider and support the sidewall better at lower pressures.
  • Shape changes too. The same tire on a wider rim sits more square. On a narrower rim, it balloons slightly rounder.
  • Compatibility. Too narrow or too wide a rim for a given tire can create poor shape, squirm, burping risk, or handling problems.
  • Always check. Rim and tire manufacturer recommendations for compatible width ranges. Tubeless fit is especially sensitive.
Section 06 — Tire Talk

On rough surfaces, lower pressure can reduce energy lost to vibration and bouncing.

More air volume lets the tire deform
without folding. That can mean
more speed, more grip, less fatigue.

Section 06 — Tire Talk

Practical
Width Ranges

The road category has moved wider. Many riders now use 28 to 32 mm because they can be fast and more comfortable.

  • Road. 28 to 32 mm is common. Some frames clear 35 mm. Many riders find 30 mm a good all-round choice.
  • Gravel. 40 to 50 mm is common. 40 to 45 mm for mixed terrain. 50 mm for rougher events if the frame allows.
  • Cyclocross racing. UCI rule limits to 33 mm for sanctioned events. Non-race riding can use wider if the frame clears it.
  • Mountain reference. Mountain bike tires use inches. A 50 mm gravel tire is roughly 2 inches. A 2.4 inch MTB tire is roughly 61 mm.
Section 06 — Tire Talk

Tubes And
Tubeless

Three setups. Three different maintenance stories.

  • Butyl tubes. Cheap, reliable, familiar. The default for decades. Still entirely legitimate for most riding.
  • Latex tubes. Reduce rolling resistance. Require more frequent inflation and careful installation. More delicate than butyl.
  • Tubeless. Sealant handles small punctures automatically. Lower pressure without pinch flat risk. More setup fuss. Modern road tubeless is better, but rim fit and installation quality still matter a lot.
  • Tubeless backup. Still carry a tube. Plugs handle larger punctures. A tube handles the day sealant gives up.
Section 06 — Tire Talk

Rolling resistance is energy lost as the tire flexes, rebounds, slips, and vibrates through the real world.

Lab testing is controlled. Real rides add surface, grip, handling, rider confidence, and puncture risk. Use the data as a comparison, not the only decision.

Section 06 — Tire Talk

How Much Should
You Care?

A tire that makes you brake less, corner better, and arrive less beaten up is often the faster tire.

  • Pick the right tread first. Wrong tread on the wrong surface wastes more energy than rolling resistance numbers can save.
  • Then the right width. More volume at the right pressure reduces energy lost to vibration and bouncing.
  • Then the right pressure. Ideal pressure depends on tire size, system weight, surface, rim, and speed. Use a guide, not folklore.
  • Then care about watts. Rolling resistance matters most for long events where small losses repeat for hours.

Section 07 — Adjustments and Diagnosis

Do not turn screws
until you know what problem
the screw solves.

First identify the system, the symptom, and what changed recently: wheel removal, travel, crash, new chain, dirty drivetrain, dead battery, cable stretch, pad wear, bent hanger.

Section 07 — Adjustments

Cable Shifting Drifts Through

  • Cable tension loosening over time
  • Friction building in housing or at junctions
  • Contamination in cables or housing
  • Fix: barrel adjuster and regular cable service
 

Electronic Shifting Drifts Through

  • Initial setup or micro-adjustment position
  • Battery level or firmware state
  • Hanger alignment or physical damage
  • Fix: manufacturer app procedure and alignment check
Section 07 — Adjustments

Barrel Adjusters

Move the derailleur indexing. Small turns. Test. Repeat.

  • What they do. Fine-tune the indexed positions relative to the cassette. Barrel adjusters change cable tension without touching the anchor bolt.
  • Chain hesitates to a larger cog. Cable tension may need to increase. Turn barrel adjuster counterclockwise (out) to add tension.
  • Chain hesitates to a smaller cog. Cable tension may need to decrease. Turn barrel adjuster clockwise (in) to reduce tension.
  • What it will not fix. Bent hanger, wrong limit screw, worn chain, or incompatible parts. Fix those first, then index.
Section 07 — Adjustments

Electronic micro-adjustment is the barrel adjuster idea with buttons.

Use the maker's procedure.
Do not guess.

Micro-adjustment changes the derailleur's indexed position. It does not fix chain wear, bent hangers, incompatible cassettes, wrong chain length, or a derailleur that took a parking-lot nap.

Section 07 — Adjustments

Disc Brake Rub

First question: rotor, caliper, pad, or wheel seating?

  • Check wheel seating first. Make sure the wheel is fully seated and the thru-axle is tight. Many "rub" calls end here.
  • Constant rub. The caliper may be off-center. Loosen mounting bolts, squeeze the brake lever to center the caliper, tighten evenly, check clearance.
  • Pulsing rub once per wheel revolution. The rotor may be slightly bent. Rotor truing tools or careful hand straightening at low spots.
  • Goal. Caliper parallel to the rotor with even clearance on both sides. Adequate lever feel throughout the pull.
Section 07 — Adjustments

Brake Pad Wear

Longer lever pull, later bite point, noise, and weak braking are clues.

  • Hydraulic systems. Self-adjust for pad wear until they do not feel right anymore. Mushy feel or excessive lever travel usually means pads or fluid.
  • Mechanical systems. May require pad or cable adjustment as pads wear down. Lever travel changes visibly.
  • Contamination. Oil, chain lube, or sealant on pads or rotors destroys braking performance. Contaminated pads often need replacement.
  • Rule. If braking feels mushy, inconsistent, contaminated, or underpowered, stop riding hard and inspect it. Brakes are not a "ride and see" system.

Section 08 — Pre-Ride Checks

Pre-ride checks are not a full tune. They are a quick scan for common failure points.

The best repair is the one
you do before everyone
is waiting.

Section 08 — Pre-Ride

Air

Squeeze is not a pressure gauge, but it can catch a flat or slow leak. Use a gauge for actual pressure.

  • Check pressure. Use a gauge. Target pressure depends on tire size, rider weight, rim width, and surface.
  • Look for cuts. Sidewall cuts, embedded glass, or visible casing threads mean the tire is close to failing.
  • Spin the tire. Look for wobble, bulge, or any foreign object embedded in the tread.
  • Tubeless: notice sealant weeping. Dried sealant around a spot means the tire has healed a puncture. Note the spot. It may not hold indefinitely.
Section 08 — Pre-Ride

Brakes

Squeeze both levers hard. Spin both wheels. Listen for rub. Confirm the bike stops before the ride asks.

  • Squeeze firmly. If the lever hits the bar, something is wrong. Not a "ride and see" situation.
  • Listen for rub. A constant scrape is caliper alignment. A rhythmic scrape is a bent rotor or out-of-true rim.
  • Mushy or spongy feel. Hydraulic: air in the line or low fluid. Mechanical: cable stretch or pad wear. Both need attention before descending.
  • Weak braking. Contaminated pads, glazed rotors, or pad wear. Do not trust weak brakes on a group ride.
Section 08 — Pre-Ride

Wheels And Axles

A slightly mis-seated wheel can create brake rub and poor handling without feeling obviously wrong.

  • Check thru-axles. Fully closed and finger-tight to spec. A loose thru-axle can feel fine until it does not.
  • Check quick releases. Cam closed firmly. Properly tensioned, not just clicked shut.
  • Wiggle for bearing play. Grab the wheel at the rim and push side to side. Any lateral movement means loose bearings or a loose axle.
  • Spin for wobble. Significant side-to-side movement in the rim means the wheel is out of true. Minor wobble is normal. Major wobble is not.
Section 08 — Pre-Ride

Chain And
Drivetrain

A quick spin and listen covers most pre-ride drivetrain checks.

  • Backpedal. A dry, grinding, or clicking chain means it needs lube or there is a stiff link.
  • Listen. Drivetrain should be quiet. Noise is information. Identify the source before dismissing it.
  • Shift once. Make sure the system responds normally. Electronic: check battery indicator if available.
  • Look at the chain. Off the pulley, obviously dry, or stiff links are visible problems. Fix them before the group ride.
Section 08 — Pre-Ride

Cockpit And
Contact Points

Do not torque-randomly before a ride, but do inspect. Carbon parts need correct torque and often carbon assembly paste.

  • Bars straight. Centered with the front wheel. Rotate the bars to check — they should be where you left them.
  • Stem tight. Try to rotate the bars relative to the fork. Any movement means loose stem bolts.
  • Seatpost and saddle secure. Try to rotate the saddle by hand. Try to push the post down. Neither should move.
  • Pedals normal. Spin each pedal. Grinding or lateral play means bearing service is due.
Section 08 — Pre-Ride

Carry The Right
Small Things

The goal is not to bring the garage. It is to bring the most likely ride-saving fixes.

  • Tube or plugs. Tubeless riders still need a backup plan. Sealant handles some. Plugs handle more. A tube handles the rest.
  • Inflation. Mini pump or CO2. Know how to use it before you need it at mile 40.
  • Multi-tool. Allen keys, torx bits if your bike has them, and a chain tool.
  • Chain quick link. Can save a snapped chain. Carry one that matches your chain speed.
  • ID, card, phone. Not mechanical, but part of being prepared to handle a bad day.

Chainstay Length

Chainstay length is bottom bracket center to rear axle center. Short stays feel snappy and immediate. Longer stays calm the rear of the bike, help stability at speed, create room for tires and mud, and change how deliberately the rear wheel follows through corners.

Effective Top Tube

Effective top tube is the horizontal distance from the seat tube centerline to the head tube centerline. An older fit clue. Useful, but less clean than stack and reach. Two bikes can have the same top tube and feel different if stack, reach, seat angle, stem, and bar reach all differ.

K

Head Tube Length

Head tube length is one way a frame creates stack. A longer head tube can make an upright position cleaner and more structurally straightforward. A shorter head tube gives more room to create a low race position without raising the front end.

Frame Geometry
L

Bottom Bracket Drop

Bottom bracket drop is how far the BB center sits below the wheel axle centerline. More drop lowers center of gravity for stability. Less drop raises the BB for pedal clearance on rough terrain, off-camber turns, and remounts. Must be read with tire size because larger tires raise the whole bike.

Geometry Deep Dive

A gravel bike can have a slacker head angle but still steer predictably because fork offset and tire size tune trail. A longer wheelbase can feel stable, but if the rider is too stretched, it can still feel bad.

Road

Wants efficiency

  • Low front end, aggressive position
  • Quick steering, short rear center
  • Short wheelbase
  • Narrow to moderate tires (28–32 mm)
  • High BB drop, low center of gravity
Gravel

Wants composure

  • Taller front end, longer front center
  • Longer wheelbase for rough descents
  • More tire volume and clearance
  • More stability on loose surfaces
  • More BB drop to manage bigger tires
Cyclocross

Wants repeatable violence

  • Race-tall, quick enough to snap through tape
  • Higher BB for mud and remount clearance
  • Tires shaped by UCI 33 mm rule
  • Stable in mud, sharp in tight turns
  • Built for repeated accelerations
Closing

What You Should
Leave With

Not memorized numbers. A working model for the bike under you.

  • Name the part. You now have a language for what you are looking at and what it does.
  • Understand the tradeoff. Every geometry number, material choice, and drivetrain decision is a tradeoff, not a verdict.
  • Diagnose before adjusting. Identify the system and symptom before turning anything.
  • Ask better questions at the shop. That is the real goal. A clear symptom gets a better fix.
Closing

What Comes Next

Maintenance gets easier when the rider knows the baseline.

  • Ride it. Pay attention to what it sounds and feels like when everything is right. That is your reference point.
  • Listen to it. New noise means something changed. Find the source before it becomes a bigger issue.
  • Clean it. Clean bikes are easier to inspect, easier to diagnose, and more fun to ride.
  • Measure before guessing. Chain wear, tire pressure, saddle height. Numbers beat feelings.
