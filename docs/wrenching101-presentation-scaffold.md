# Wrenching 101 Presentation Scaffold

Purpose: full content and hierarchy scaffold for a beginner-facing Wrenching 101 slide deck.

Audience: mostly beginners, specifically riders who ride confidently but have not spent much time learning the mechanical side.

Voice: Mettle Cycling voice. Welcoming, sharp, no gatekeeping, no fake shop mysticism. Assume intelligence. Normalize not knowing. Keep the room moving.

Use this document as the content map for Claude to build the presentation. Slide copy is intentionally fuller than final deck copy so it can be edited down during design. Keep the deck generous enough to preserve context, then trim only where repetition, pacing, or slide density gets in the way.

## Deck Rules

- Keep headlines short, confident, and slightly wry.
- Avoid "beginner-friendly" framing. This room is not remedial. The bikes are just complicated.
- Use teaching notes for nuance. Keep slide text direct.
- Use citations in presenter notes or the reference section, not as visual clutter.
- Do not over-explain the joke.
- For display text using New Athletic 54, keep ASCII only and avoid contractions.
- No em dashes.

## Recommended Deck Shape

- Intro: 4 to 6 slides
- Eight curriculum sections: 5 to 8 slides each
- Geometry deep dive: 14 to 18 slides
- Closing: 2 to 3 slides
- Reference section: appendix, not presented unless needed

Total working range: 70 to 90 slides before trimming. Shorter is fine if the hierarchy stays intact and the teaching context survives.

# SECTION 1: BIKE TYPES AND GEOMETRY

## Slide 6: Same Shape, Different Job

Slide copy:

Road, gravel, and cyclocross bikes share a silhouette.

They do not share the same assignment.

Presenter notes:

These bikes all look like drop-bar bikes. The differences are in the job description: speed, surface, handling, tire clearance, rider position, and how much chaos the bike is expected to absorb.

## Slide 7: Road Bikes

Slide copy:

Road bikes prioritize efficiency, low weight, sharp steering, and aerodynamic position.

Typical tire range: about 28 to 32 mm, with newer race bikes often clearing up to 32 mm.

Presenter notes:

Road geometry is usually shorter, lower, and quicker. The rider is placed in a position that can be aerodynamic and efficient for sustained speed. The tradeoff is that the bike may feel nervous on loose surfaces or rough roads compared with gravel or cyclocross bikes. The S-Works Tarmac SL8 is a useful modern reference because it is a pure race bike with 32 mm tire clearance and published geometry across sizes. [R2]

Visual direction:

Use side-profile S-Works Tarmac SL8 visual reference.

## Slide 8: Gravel Bikes

Slide copy:

Gravel bikes add tire volume, stability, clearance, and all-day control.

Typical tire range: about 38 to 50 mm, depending on frame, wheel, and intended use.

Presenter notes:

Gravel is not one category. Some gravel bikes are road bikes with bigger tires. Some are nearly drop-bar mountain bikes. The common thread is that they give the rider more air volume, more stability, and more confidence when the surface gets rough. The Scott Addict Gravel is a good example of fast gravel thinking: aero cues, 45 mm clearance, comfort features, and compatibility with 1x or 2x drivetrains. [R3]

Visual direction:

Use side-profile Scott Addict Gravel visual reference.

## Slide 9: Cyclocross Bikes

Slide copy:

Cyclocross bikes are race tools for short, violent laps: grass, mud, barriers, accelerations, remounts.

They are not just gravel bikes with bad manners.

Presenter notes:

Cyclocross geometry balances quick handling with enough clearance and stability to survive mud, off-camber turns, dismounts, remounts, and repeated accelerations. UCI cyclocross racing limits tire width to 33 mm, which heavily shapes the category. Many CX bikes feel taller and more urgent than gravel bikes because they are designed for repeated race efforts, not loaded all-day exploration. [R4], [R5]

Visual direction:

Use side-profile Stevens Super Prestige visual reference.

## Slide 10: What Geometry Really Means

Slide copy:

Geometry is how a frame places your body between two wheels.

Then it decides how those wheels behave.

Presenter notes:

Most riders think geometry is a size chart. It is more than that. Stack and reach affect fit. Head angle, fork offset, and trail affect steering. Wheelbase, chainstay length, and front center affect stability and cornering. Bottom bracket drop affects center of gravity and pedal clearance. The magic is not in one number. It is the combination. [R1], [R6], [R8]

## Slide 11: Road, Gravel, CX At A Glance

Slide copy:

Road: lower, shorter, quicker, faster on pavement.

Gravel: longer, calmer, more tire, more surface range.

Cyclocross: tall enough, sharp enough, built for repeated race chaos.

Presenter notes:

Use this as the "do not memorize numbers yet" slide. A rider should leave able to feel the direction of the categories before reading exact geometry.

# SECTION 2: FRAME MATERIALS

## Slide 12: Material Is Not Destiny

Slide copy:

Carbon is not automatically fast.

Steel is not automatically magic.

Aluminum is not automatically harsh.

Design decides what the material is allowed to become.

Presenter notes:

Material matters, but it is not the whole bike. Tube shape, wall thickness, layup, tire size, seatpost design, fork design, and intended use all change ride quality. A bad carbon frame can ride badly. A smart aluminum frame can ride beautifully. We are talking tendencies, not laws. [R10], [R11]

## Slide 13: Aluminum

Slide copy:

Light, stiff, relatively affordable, and very good at being a bike.

Presenter notes:

Aluminum frames are usually welded from shaped and butted tubes. Hydroforming lets brands create complex tube shapes for stiffness, clearance, and visual design. Aluminum has a strong stiffness-to-weight story and remains common on budget, race, commuter, gravel, and mountain bikes. The old reputation for harshness came from oversized, stiff tube designs and narrower tires. Modern aluminum can be much better, especially with larger tires and smarter shaping.

Cost and benefit:

Lower cost than carbon. Often lighter than steel at a given price. Good privateer race choice. Harder to repair than steel, and fatigue life matters over a very long timeline.

## Slide 14: Steel

Slide copy:

Durable, repairable, tunable, and still very much alive.

Presenter notes:

Steel dominated bicycle frames for much of cycling history. Modern steel is usually chromoly or higher-end air-hardened tubing, welded, brazed, or lugged. Because steel is strong and dense, builders can use smaller diameter tubes with thinner walls. The ride feel people describe as "springy" comes from tube design and frame construction, not a supernatural property hidden inside the metal.

Cost and benefit:

Can be affordable or extremely custom. Easy to repair compared with carbon or aluminum. Usually heavier than carbon or aluminum for performance road use. Excellent for custom fit, touring, all-road, commuting, and riders who value longevity.

## Slide 15: Carbon

Slide copy:

Light, shapeable, tunable, expensive, and very easy to market badly.

Presenter notes:

Carbon frames are made from carbon fiber plies embedded in resin. Layup direction matters because carbon is directional: it can be very stiff in one direction and more compliant in another depending on fiber orientation. That lets engineers tune stiffness, comfort, aerodynamics, and weight in ways metal tubes cannot match as easily. It also means two frames that look similar can ride very differently.

Cost and benefit:

Best material for high-end race performance when designed well. Excellent shape freedom. Expensive tooling. Crash damage can be hard to inspect. Repair is possible in many cases, but it is specialized.

## Slide 16: Compliance Without Sorcery

Slide copy:

Comfort comes from the system:

frame, fork, seatpost, tires, pressure, saddle, bar tape, and position.

Presenter notes:

Compliance is the controlled flex or vibration management of the bike. Tire volume and pressure often change comfort more than frame material alone. Frame design still matters, especially at the seatpost, fork, and rear triangle, but riders should resist buying material myths as if they are fit advice.

# SECTION 3: FIT BASICS

## Slide 17: A Good Fit Has A Job

Slide copy:

A good position lets you breathe, steer, pedal, brake, and repeat the ride tomorrow.

Presenter notes:

Fit is not just "comfort" and race fit is not just "lower." A good fit supports the ride someone is trying to do. The same rider might need a different position for a criterium, a gravel race, a long endurance road ride, or a casual recovery spin.

## Slide 18: What Good Position Means

Slide copy:

Stable hips.

Quiet shoulders.

Soft elbows.

Hands that do not go numb.

Knees that track cleanly.

Power that does not require a wrestling match.

Presenter notes:

This is the human version of the fit chart. If a rider looks like they are bracing against the bike, the bike is not supporting them. Common signals include neck pain, hand numbness, saddle discomfort, hot feet, knee pain, and lower back fatigue. [R23], [R26]

## Slide 19: Everything Starts At The Bottom Bracket

Slide copy:

The bottom bracket is the coordinate system.

Saddle first. Cockpit second.

Presenter notes:

Stack and reach are measured from the bottom bracket because the bottom bracket is where pedaling happens. Saddle height and saddle fore-aft position establish the rider relative to the pedals. Once that is stable, the cockpit is adjusted to meet the rider. Do not fix a saddle problem with a stem if the saddle is wrong first. [R9], [R25]

## Slide 20: Endurance Fit Vs Race Fit

Slide copy:

Endurance fit: more upright, more sustainable, less aerodynamic.

Race fit: lower, longer, faster when the rider can support it.

Presenter notes:

Endurance road frames often use a taller stack and shorter reach than race frames, which makes it easier to place the bar higher without a tower of spacers. A race fit is not better. It is more demanding. A low position that collapses breathing, loads the hands, or makes descending sketchy is not fast in the useful sense.

## Slide 21: Weight Distribution

Slide copy:

The bike should carry you across saddle, pedals, and hands.

Hands are for control, not bodyweight storage.

Presenter notes:

Too much forward weight can create hand numbness, neck tension, and an unstable feeling on descents. Too much rearward weight can make the front end wander or feel light. Saddle setback, reach, bar height, stem length, and rider posture all affect the balance. [R26], [R27]

## Slide 22: Stack Height

Slide copy:

Stack is how high the frame lets the front end start.

More stack usually means easier bar height.

Presenter notes:

Endurance frames tend to provide more stack for a given size. Race frames often start lower, expecting the rider to tolerate more bar drop. Spacers and stems can modify the final cockpit, but they cannot make every frame fit every rider without changing handling, aesthetics, or both.

## Slide 23: Where Problems Show Up

Slide copy:

Hands, neck, knees, saddle, feet, back.

The bike leaves clues.

Presenter notes:

Common clues:

- Front knee pain can come from saddle too low or too far forward.
- Back of knee pain can come from saddle too high.
- Hand numbness can come from too much hand pressure, saddle tilt, reach, posture, glove fit, or bar setup.
- Saddle discomfort can be saddle shape, width, tilt, height, or a stability problem elsewhere.
- Lower back pain can be reach, bar drop, hip mobility, saddle stability, or fatigue.

Use this as diagnosis, not medical advice. Persistent pain belongs with a fitter or clinician.

# SECTION 4: DRIVETRAIN TYPES

## Slide 24: The Drivetrain Is A Team Sport

Slide copy:

Chain, cassette, chainrings, derailleur, shifters.

They wear, move, and complain as a system.

Presenter notes:

This section gives people the map before the maintenance section. A drivetrain is not one part. If one part is dirty, worn, bent, misadjusted, or incompatible, the whole system can feel bad.

## Slide 25: Very Short Drivetrain History

Slide copy:

Early bikes had fewer gears, fewer choices, and much more character-building.

Modern bikes have more range, more precision, and more standards to misremember.

Presenter notes:

Early 1900s bikes had limited gearing. Derailleur systems gradually became normal in racing and recreational bikes. Through the late 20th century, road drivetrains moved toward indexed shifting and more rear cogs. By the 2000s, 2x10 road drivetrains were common. Shimano Dura-Ace went 11-speed in 2012. Electronic Di2 appeared in Dura-Ace in 2009 and expanded from there. [R12], [R13]

## Slide 26: The 2x Road Era

Slide copy:

Two rings up front.

Ten, eleven, or twelve cogs in back.

Small jumps. Big range. More moving parts.

Presenter notes:

2x drivetrains dominated road because they offer a wide range with relatively tight jumps between gears. That matters when maintaining cadence on pavement. The cost is added complexity: front shifting, cross-chaining decisions, trim or electronic compensation, and more adjustment points.

## Slide 27: The 1x Shift

Slide copy:

One ring. Wider cassette. Fewer decisions.

The front derailleur left the chat.

Presenter notes:

1x took off in mountain biking, then gravel and cyclocross, because clutched derailleurs and narrow-wide chainrings improved chain retention. SRAM X-SYNC chainrings use alternating tooth profiles to better control the chain, especially in rough or muddy conditions. 1x is simpler to think about and less likely to drop a chain at the front. The tradeoff is bigger jumps between some gears and sometimes less ideal cadence on fast pavement. [R14], [R15]

## Slide 28: Electronic Shifting

Slide copy:

Electronic shifting replaces cable tension with motors, batteries, buttons, and firmware.

It is not magic. It is just very good at repeating itself.

Presenter notes:

Shimano Di2 brought road electronic shifting into the mainstream at the high end. SRAM eTap and AXS popularized wireless shifting and app-based setup. Electronic shifting can reduce cable contamination issues and improve shift consistency, but it adds batteries, charging, pairing, and diagnostics. It changes the troubleshooting tree, not the need to understand the bike.

## Slide 29: Gear Ratios And Jumps

Slide copy:

Range tells you how easy and hard the gears get.

Jumps tell you how smooth the steps feel.

Presenter notes:

A wide-range 1x cassette may give plenty of climbing gear but larger jumps between cogs. A 2x drivetrain can offer similar total range with smaller cadence changes. The right answer depends on terrain, riding style, and tolerance for cadence gaps. For gravel and CX, simplicity and chain retention can beat perfect cadence spacing. For road, tight jumps often matter more.

## Slide 30: Why 1x Drops Chains Less

Slide copy:

Fewer front shifts. More chain control. Less drama.

Presenter notes:

A 1x setup removes the front shift, where many dropped chains happen. Narrow-wide teeth and clutch derailleurs help the chain stay engaged over bumps. A 2x can still be excellent, and when something goes wrong mid-ride it may be easier to limp home by avoiding one ring or one derailleur position. The point is tradeoffs, not team sports.

# SECTION 5: DRIVETRAIN MAINTENANCE

## Slide 31: Clean Chains Are Not A Personality Type

Slide copy:

The chain is a wear part.

Dirt turns it into expensive sandpaper.

Presenter notes:

Chain maintenance is not about being precious. It is about reducing wear across the chain, cassette, and chainrings. A dirty chain accelerates wear because contamination works into the rollers and pins. The worse the contamination, the faster the drivetrain becomes a group project with a receipt.

## Slide 32: Lube Basics

Slide copy:

Wet lube: durable, messy, better in wet conditions.

Dry lube: cleaner, less durable, better in dry conditions.

All-condition lube: compromise by design.

Presenter notes:

Traditional drip lubes work by leaving lubricant inside the chain. The outside of the chain should be wiped clean because external lube attracts grit. Colorado dryness makes this tricky: dust sticks to wet lube, and very dry conditions can strip lighter lubes quickly.

## Slide 33: Wax Basics

Slide copy:

Wax works because it dries solid and sheds contamination instead of collecting it.

Presenter notes:

Hot wax and drip wax aim to put lubricant inside the chain without leaving an oily exterior. A properly waxed chain runs clean to the touch, resists contamination, and can extend drivetrain life when maintained. Silca recommends drip wax top-ups or re-waxing after clean riding intervals around 150 to 200 miles depending on conditions and product. [R16], [R17]

## Slide 34: Cleaning A Traditional Lube Drivetrain

Slide copy:

Degrease.

Scrub.

Rinse carefully.

Dry completely.

Apply the right lube.

Wipe the outside clean.

Presenter notes:

Use citrus degreaser or bike-specific degreaser carefully. Scrub chain, cassette, jockey wheels, and chainrings. Rinse without blasting water into bearings. Dry the chain completely before lubing. Apply lube to the rollers, let it penetrate, then wipe the exterior. The chain should be lubricated inside, not wearing a wet jacket.

## Slide 35: Traditional Lube Watchouts

Slide copy:

More lube is not more maintenance.

It is usually more dirt.

Presenter notes:

Watch for black paste on the chain, noisy shifting, squeaking, stiff links, and chain stretch. In dry, dusty places, a traditionally lubed chain may need attention around 100 miles or sooner depending on conditions. The right interval depends on dust, weather, product, and how clean the chain started.

## Slide 36: Maintaining A Waxed Chain

Slide copy:

Keep it clean.

Top it up.

Re-wax before it gets loud.

Presenter notes:

For wax, initial cleaning matters most. Factory grease must be removed before hot waxing or drip wax conversion. After that, maintenance is easier: wipe after wet rides, top up with compatible drip wax, or re-immerse in hot wax. With hot wax, the clean chain can go straight back into melted wax after wiping debris off, assuming it has not been contaminated with oil. [R16]

## Slide 37: Chain Wear Is Cassette Wear

Slide copy:

A worn chain teaches the cassette bad habits.

Then the cassette gets expensive.

Presenter notes:

Use a chain checker. Replace chains before they wear far enough to damage cassettes and chainrings. This is especially important on modern 11-speed, 12-speed, and 13-speed systems where parts are narrower and more expensive.

# SECTION 6: TIRE TALK

## Slide 38: Tires Are Suspension

Slide copy:

Your tires are the first suspension system on the bike.

Pressure is not a bravery contest.

Presenter notes:

Tires affect comfort, grip, rolling resistance, puncture resistance, cornering, and confidence. For many riders, tire choice and pressure change the ride more than frame material.

## Slide 39: Folding Tire Construction

Slide copy:

Bead. Casing. Rubber. Tread. Protection layer.

Simple parts, complicated behavior.

Presenter notes:

Folding bead tires use a flexible bead, usually aramid or similar fiber, instead of a rigid wire bead. The casing gives the tire its structure. Rubber compound controls grip and wear. Tread shape controls surface interaction. Protection layers reduce punctures but can add stiffness and rolling resistance.

## Slide 40: TPI Without The Bro Science

Slide copy:

TPI means threads per inch.

Higher TPI can feel faster and more supple, but less rubber can mean less protection.

Presenter notes:

Higher TPI casings can flex more easily, reducing energy lost to casing deformation and improving ride feel. But casing design is not just a number. Rubber thickness, puncture layers, compound, sidewall construction, and intended use all matter. A delicate race tire is not automatically the right tire for chunky gravel.

## Slide 41: Tread Comes First

Slide copy:

Pavement: slick or file tread.

Fine gravel: low knobs, fast center.

Mixed gravel: supported shoulder knobs.

Chunky gravel: volume and protection.

Mud: open tread that clears.

Presenter notes:

Choose tread for surface first. Rolling resistance is useful, but the wrong tread on the wrong surface is not efficient. A fast slick on loose dirt can waste energy through slipping, braking, and caution. A heavy mud tire on clean pavement wastes energy through tread deformation.

## Slide 42: Width And Rim Width

Slide copy:

Tire width is not just the number printed on the sidewall.

The rim changes the shape.

Presenter notes:

Internal rim width affects inflated tire width and profile. Wider rims can make a tire measure wider and support the sidewall better at lower pressures. Too narrow or too wide a rim for a given tire can create poor shape, squirm, burping risk, or handling problems. Always check tire and rim compatibility.

## Slide 43: Bigger Tires, Lower Pressure

Slide copy:

More air volume lets the tire deform without folding.

That can mean more speed, more grip, and less fatigue.

Presenter notes:

Modern research and testing have challenged the old "harder is faster" idea. On rough surfaces, lower pressure can reduce energy lost to vibration and bouncing. Schwalbe explains that wider tires can roll better because a wider tire forms a shorter contact patch at the same pressure, reducing deformation. BikeRadar and Bicycling both stress that the ideal pressure depends on tire size, system weight, surface, rim, and speed. [R21], [R22], [R23]

## Slide 44: Practical Width Ranges

Slide copy:

Road: 28 to 32 mm is common, with some frames clearing 35 mm.

Gravel: 40 to 50 mm is common.

Mountain bike tires use inches. A 50 mm gravel tire is roughly 2 inches.

Presenter notes:

The road category has moved wider. Many riders now use 28, 30, or 32 mm tires because they can be fast and more comfortable. Gravel riders often choose 40 to 45 mm for mixed terrain and 50 mm for rougher events if the frame clears it. Cyclocross racing stays shaped by the 33 mm UCI rule, though non-UCI riding can use wider tires if the frame allows. [R2], [R3], [R5]

## Slide 45: Tubes And Tubeless

Slide copy:

Butyl tubes: cheap, reliable, familiar.

Latex tubes: faster, lighter feel, more delicate.

Tubeless: sealant, lower pressure, fewer pinch flats, more setup fuss.

Presenter notes:

Butyl tubes were the default for decades. Latex tubes reduce rolling resistance but require more frequent inflation and careful installation. Tubeless first took hold in mountain biking because pinch flats and low pressure mattered there. Road tubeless took longer because higher pressure makes sealing and rim fit more demanding. Modern road tubeless is better, but setup quality still matters.

## Slide 46: Rolling Resistance

Slide copy:

Rolling resistance is energy lost as the tire flexes, rebounds, slips, and vibrates through the real world.

Presenter notes:

Bicycle Rolling Resistance is useful because it provides controlled comparisons between tires. Use it to compare casing, compound, puncture protection, and pressure. Do not use it as the only decision. Lab testing is controlled. Real rides add surface, handling, grip, rider confidence, aero interaction, and puncture risk. [R24], [R22]

## Slide 47: How Much Should You Care?

Slide copy:

Pick the right tread first.

Then the right width.

Then the right pressure.

Then care about watts.

Presenter notes:

For long events, rolling resistance matters because small losses repeat for hours. For most riders, the biggest win is choosing the correct tire for the surface and running sensible pressure. A tire that makes you brake less, corner better, and arrive less beaten up is often the faster tire.

# SECTION 7: ADJUSTMENTS AND DIAGNOSIS

## Slide 48: Diagnosis Before Adjustment

Slide copy:

Do not turn screws until you know what problem the screw solves.

Presenter notes:

This is the most important maintenance habit. Bikes invite random quarter-turns. Resist. First identify the system, the symptom, and what changed recently: wheel removal, travel, crash, new chain, dirty drivetrain, dead battery, cable stretch, pad wear, bent hanger.

## Slide 49: Shifting: Cable Vs Electronic

Slide copy:

Cable shifting usually drifts through tension, friction, housing, and contamination.

Electronic shifting usually drifts through setup, battery, firmware, hanger alignment, or physical damage.

Presenter notes:

Cable systems use cable tension to place the derailleur. Electronic systems use motor positions. Both still depend on a straight hanger, clean drivetrain, compatible parts, and proper limit settings. Electronic shifting is precise, but not immune to bent hangers or worn chains.

## Slide 50: Barrel Adjusters

Slide copy:

Barrel adjusters move the derailleur indexing.

Small turns. Test. Repeat.

Presenter notes:

Park Tool describes indexing as aligning the guide pulley with each cog. Barrel adjusters fine-tune the indexed positions relative to the cassette. If the chain hesitates moving to a larger cog, cable tension may need to increase. If it hesitates moving to a smaller cog, cable tension may need to decrease. But if the hanger is bent or limit screws are wrong, barrel adjustment will not save it. [R18]

## Slide 51: Micro Adjustment

Slide copy:

Electronic micro-adjustment is the barrel adjuster idea with buttons.

Presenter notes:

Use the drivetrain maker's procedure. Do not guess. Micro-adjustment changes the derailleur's indexed position. It does not fix chain wear, bent hangers, incompatible cassettes, wrong chain length, or a derailleur that took a parking-lot nap.

## Slide 52: Disc Brake Rub

Slide copy:

First question: rotor, caliper, pad, or wheel seating?

Presenter notes:

Check that the wheel is seated and the thru-axle is tight. Spin the wheel and listen. If rub is constant, the caliper may be off-center. If rub pulses once per wheel revolution, the rotor may be slightly bent. Caliper centering usually means loosening mounting bolts, squeezing the brake lever to center the caliper, tightening evenly, then checking clearance. Park Tool notes the goal is a caliper parallel to the rotor with even gaps and adequate lever feel. [R19]

## Slide 53: Brake Pad Wear

Slide copy:

Longer lever pull, later bite point, noise, and weak braking are clues.

Presenter notes:

Pad wear changes lever feel and braking performance. Hydraulic systems may self-adjust for pad wear until they do not feel right anymore. Mechanical systems may require pad or cable adjustment. If braking feels mushy, inconsistent, contaminated, or underpowered, stop riding hard and inspect it.

## Slide 54: Home Fix, Advanced Fix, Shop Fix

Slide copy:

Home: clean, lube, inflate, inspect, micro-adjust, center minor rub.

Advanced: cables, housing, chain sizing, brake bleeding, hanger alignment.

Shop: structural damage, bearing service, hydraulic problems, seized parts, mystery noises with consequences.

Presenter notes:

Give people permission to stop. Knowing when not to wrench is a maintenance skill. A good shop visit with a clear symptom is better than six random fixes that make the original problem harder to diagnose.

## Slide 55: Zinn Rule

Slide copy:

Use a real reference.

Memory is not a torque spec.

Presenter notes:

Zinn and the Art of Road Bike Maintenance is a strong reference for adjustment, maintenance, and troubleshooting categories. Park Tool's repair help pages are also excellent for step-by-step process. The important habit is using procedure, not folklore. [R18], [R19], [R20]

# SECTION 8: PRE-RIDE CHECKS

## Slide 56: Two Minutes Before The Ride

Slide copy:

The best repair is the one you do before everyone is waiting.

Presenter notes:

Pre-ride checks are not a full tune. They are a quick scan for common failure points. The goal is to catch the obvious thing before it becomes a group ride incident.

## Slide 57: Air

Slide copy:

Check pressure.

Look for cuts.

Spin the tire.

Notice sealant weeping.

Presenter notes:

Squeeze is not a pressure gauge, but it can catch a flat or slow leak. Use a gauge for actual pressure. Look for sidewall cuts, embedded glass, casing threads, or sealant that has dried around a puncture.

## Slide 58: Brakes

Slide copy:

Squeeze both levers hard.

Spin both wheels.

Listen for rub.

Confirm the bike stops before the ride asks.

Presenter notes:

Park Tool recommends squeezing brakes firmly during a quick pre-ride inspection. Mushy feel, excessive lever travel, scraping, or weak braking should be handled before the ride. [R20]

## Slide 59: Wheels And Axles

Slide copy:

Check thru-axles or quick releases.

Wiggle for bearing play.

Spin for wobble.

Presenter notes:

Make sure wheels are seated. A slightly mis-seated wheel can create brake rub and poor handling. Bearing play, loose axles, or wobbling wheels are not "ride and see" issues.

## Slide 60: Chain And Drivetrain

Slide copy:

Backpedal.

Listen.

Shift once.

Look at the chain.

Presenter notes:

You are looking for dry noise, stiff links, obvious contamination, a chain off the pulley, a battery issue, or shifting that got knocked out in transit.

## Slide 61: Cockpit And Contact Points

Slide copy:

Bars straight.

Stem tight.

Seatpost secure.

Saddle secure.

Pedals normal.

Presenter notes:

Do not torque-randomly before a ride, but do inspect. If the bars rotate, the seatpost slips, or the saddle moves, the ride is not ready. Carbon parts need correct torque and often carbon assembly paste.

## Slide 62: Carry The Right Small Things

Slide copy:

Tube or plugs.

Inflation.

Multi-tool.

Chain quick link.

ID, card, phone.

Presenter notes:

Tubeless riders should still know their backup plan. Sealant handles some punctures. Plugs handle larger ones. A tube handles the day sealant gives up. A quick link can save a snapped chain. The goal is not to bring the garage. It is to bring the most likely ride-saving fixes.

# GEOMETRY DEEP DIVE

Use this section after the eight curriculum chapters or as an appendix that can be pulled into the main deck. These slides expand the current site diagram. The measurements follow `docs/frame-geometry-research.md`.

## Slide 63: Reading A Geometry Chart

Slide copy:

A geometry chart is not a personality test.

It is a map of fit, weight distribution, and handling choices.

Presenter notes:

Teach the chart as relationships. One number rarely explains a bike. The same trail number can be created with different head angles, offsets, and tire sizes. The same reach can feel different with different stack, seat tube angle, stem, bar reach, and saddle position.

## Slide 64: A - Stack

Slide copy:

Stack is the vertical distance from the bottom bracket to the top of the head tube.

Higher stack makes a higher front end easier.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Stack affects how upright or low the rider can be without relying heavily on spacers or stem rise. Higher stack usually supports comfort, breathing, and control for longer rides. Lower stack makes it easier to create a low, aerodynamic race position.

Road:

Race road bikes often use lower stack for aggressive bar drop. Endurance road bikes increase stack for sustainable posture.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes often use more stack to improve control on rough surfaces and reduce fatigue during long rides.

Cyclocross:

CX stack can be relatively tall because the rider needs control, repeated accelerations, dismounts, remounts, and handling while tired.

Typical ranges from current research:

Road: 515-591 mm. Gravel: 530-634 mm. Cyclocross: 526-597 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R9], [R25]

## Slide 65: B - Reach

Slide copy:

Reach is the horizontal distance from the bottom bracket to the top of the head tube.

It tells you how long the frame is before parts get involved.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Reach affects rider stretch, cockpit length, front-end loading, and how much room the rider has to move. Stem length and handlebar reach can adjust the final contact point, but frame reach sets the baseline.

Road:

Race road reach tends to support a lower, longer, more aerodynamic position.

Gravel:

Gravel reach varies widely. Many designs balance a stable front center with a shorter stem for control on rough surfaces.

Cyclocross:

CX reach needs room for aggressive handling without making remounts, accelerations, and technical corners feel stretched out.

Typical ranges:

Road: 374-402 mm. Gravel: 379-415 mm. Cyclocross: 379-405 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R9], [R25]

## Slide 66: C - Trail

Slide copy:

Trail is the front wheel self-correction number.

More trail feels calmer. Less trail feels quicker.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Trail is the horizontal distance between where the steering axis hits the ground and where the tire contacts the ground. More trail generally increases stability and self-centering. Less trail usually makes steering feel lighter and quicker. Trail depends on head tube angle, fork offset, and tire diameter.

Road:

Race road bikes often use lower trail for responsive steering at speed.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes often use more trail for confidence on loose and rough surfaces.

Cyclocross:

CX trail is often high enough for control in mud and off-camber sections, but not so slow that the bike cannot snap through tape-lined corners.

Typical ranges:

Road: 55-61 mm. Gravel: 58-71 mm. Cyclocross: 62-71 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R6], [R8]

## Slide 67: D - Fork Offset

Slide copy:

Fork offset is how far the front axle sits ahead of the steering axis.

It is one of the knobs designers use to tune trail.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

With the same head angle and tire size, more fork offset reduces trail and quickens steering feel. Less offset increases trail and calms the front end. Offset also affects front center, toe overlap, and wheelbase, so it cannot be read alone.

Road:

Road forks use offset to preserve consistent handling across frame sizes.

Gravel:

Gravel forks often pair more tire clearance and slacker head angles with offsets that keep trail in the intended range.

Cyclocross:

CX bikes often use moderate offset to balance race agility with muddy-course stability.

Typical ranges:

Road: 40-55 mm. Gravel: 46-55 mm. Cyclocross: 45-50 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R6], [R7], [R8]

## Slide 68: E - Front Center

Slide copy:

Front center is bottom bracket to front axle.

It shapes toe clearance, front-end stability, and weight balance.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Longer front center moves the front axle farther ahead of the rider, usually improving stability and reducing toe overlap. Shorter front center can feel more direct and compact but may load the front wheel differently.

Road:

Road bikes keep front center compact for quick handling and efficient road position.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes often lengthen front center for stability, larger tires, and rough-surface confidence.

Cyclocross:

CX front center needs enough clearance and stability for technical terrain while keeping the bike quick in tight race sections.

Typical ranges:

Road: 574-611 mm. Gravel: 602-651 mm. Cyclocross: 588-623 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R8]

## Slide 69: F - Wheelbase

Slide copy:

Wheelbase is axle to axle.

Longer is calmer. Shorter is more eager.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Wheelbase affects the bike's overall stability, turning radius, weight distribution, and how the bike reacts to bumps and speed. A longer wheelbase tends to feel calmer at speed and on rough terrain. A shorter wheelbase feels quicker and easier to flick through corners.

Road:

Road wheelbases are shorter to keep acceleration and direction changes sharp.

Gravel:

Gravel wheelbases are longer for rough descents, loose surfaces, bigger tires, and sometimes loaded riding.

Cyclocross:

CX wheelbase sits between the two ideas: stable enough for mud and speed changes, compact enough for tight turns and race handling.

Typical ranges:

Road: 968-1011 mm. Gravel: 1010-1078 mm. Cyclocross: 1006-1040 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R8]

## Slide 70: G - Seat Tube Angle

Slide copy:

Seat tube angle places the saddle relative to the pedals.

Steeper moves the rider forward. Slacker moves the rider back.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Seat tube angle affects saddle setback range, hip position, knee relationship to the pedal, and weight distribution. A steep angle can help keep the rider over the pedals, especially on climbs or aggressive positions. A slacker angle can place more weight rearward and change how the rider recruits muscles.

Road:

Road bikes usually stay in a tight range, with size-specific variation.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes may use similar seat angles, but the handling context changes because front center, tire size, and cockpit setup differ.

Cyclocross:

CX bikes often keep the rider in a powerful, centered position for accelerations, remounts, and handling.

Typical ranges:

Road: 73-74.5 deg. Gravel: 73-75 deg. Cyclocross: 73.2-74.5 deg.

Source cues: [R1], [R25], [R26]

## Slide 71: H - Head Tube Angle

Slide copy:

Head tube angle sets the steering axis.

Steeper is quicker. Slacker is calmer.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Head tube angle strongly affects steering feel, but it works with fork offset and tire size through trail. A steeper angle usually makes the bike respond faster. A slacker angle usually increases stability and slows steering response.

Road:

Road bikes use steeper head angles for quick handling and road-speed responsiveness.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes are often slacker to add confidence on loose, rough, or descending terrain.

Cyclocross:

CX bikes often sit between road and gravel: quick enough for tight turns, calm enough for slippery courses.

Typical ranges:

Road: 72-73.8 deg. Gravel: 69-72 deg. Cyclocross: 71-72.5 deg.

Source cues: [R1], [R6], [R8]

## Slide 72: I - Chainstay Length

Slide copy:

Chainstay length is bottom bracket to rear axle.

It controls more than heel clearance.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Chainstay length affects rear-center length, rear wheel tracking, traction, tire clearance, weight distribution, and the bike's willingness to rotate through tight turns. Short stays can feel snappy and immediate. Longer stays calm the rear of the bike, help stability at speed, create room for tires and mud, and change how deliberately the rear wheel follows through corners.

Road:

Road bikes keep chainstays short for acceleration, tight handling, and a compact wheelbase.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes use longer stays for tire clearance, stability, traction, and rough-surface composure.

Cyclocross:

CX bikes often use around 425 mm because they need tire and mud clearance, traction, and stability while still turning quickly enough for race courses.

Typical ranges:

Road: 405-413 mm. Gravel: 420-435 mm. Cyclocross: 425 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R3], [R4], [R8]

## Slide 73: J - Effective Top Tube Length

Slide copy:

Effective top tube is an older fit clue.

Useful, but less clean than stack and reach.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Effective top tube is the horizontal distance from the seat tube centerline to the head tube centerline. It helps describe cockpit length, but it is affected by seat tube angle. Two bikes can have the same top tube and feel different if stack, reach, seat angle, stem, and bar reach differ.

Road:

Road top tubes tend to support lower, longer positions, especially on race bikes.

Gravel:

Gravel top tubes can be longer on paper while using shorter stems for control.

Cyclocross:

CX top tubes balance room to move with technical handling and remount practicality.

Typical ranges:

Road: 526-581 mm. Gravel: 542-589 mm. Cyclocross: 534-578 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R9]

## Slide 74: K - Head Tube Length

Slide copy:

Head tube length is one way a frame creates stack.

Longer head tube, easier tall front end.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

Head tube length affects bar height, front-end stiffness, fit range, and spacer needs. A longer head tube can make an upright position cleaner and more structurally straightforward. A shorter head tube gives more room to create a low race position.

Road:

Race road bikes often use shorter head tubes. Endurance road bikes use taller head tubes.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes often use enough head tube length to support control and long-ride posture.

Cyclocross:

CX head tube length varies by size, but the goal is usually controllable height without making the bike sluggish.

Typical ranges:

Road: 106-184 mm. Gravel: 97-188 mm. Cyclocross: 110-170 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R2], [R9]

## Slide 75: L - Bottom Bracket Drop

Slide copy:

Bottom bracket drop is how far the pedals sit below the axles.

More drop lowers the rider. Less drop clears the ground.

Presenter notes:

What it changes:

More bottom bracket drop lowers center of gravity and can make a bike feel planted. Less drop raises the bottom bracket for pedal clearance, obstacles, off-camber terrain, and aggressive cornering where pedal strikes matter. Bottom bracket drop must be read with tire size because larger tires raise the whole bike.

Road:

Road bikes often use moderate to high drop for stability and road cornering, while still managing pedal clearance.

Gravel:

Gravel bikes often use more drop because larger tires raise the bike, and stability on loose descents matters.

Cyclocross:

CX bikes usually use less drop than gravel to preserve clearance through ruts, mud, off-camber sections, remounts, and tight technical turns.

Typical ranges:

Road: 68-77 mm. Gravel: 72-85 mm. Cyclocross: 60-70 mm.

Source cues: [R1], [R4], [R8]

## Slide 76: Geometry Is A System

Slide copy:

Do not buy one number.

Buy the relationship between numbers.

Presenter notes:

Example:

A gravel bike can have a slacker head angle but still steer predictably because fork offset and tire size tune trail. A longer wheelbase can feel stable, but if the rider is too stretched or too low, it can still feel bad. Chainstay length changes rear behavior, but front center and trail decide what the front wheel is doing at the same time.

## Slide 77: Discipline Comparison

Slide copy:

Road wants efficiency.

Gravel wants composure.

Cyclocross wants repeatable violence.

Presenter notes:

Road:

Low front end, quick steering, short rear center, short wheelbase, narrow to moderate tires.

Gravel:

Taller front end, longer front center, longer wheelbase, larger tires, more stability and clearance.

Cyclocross:

Race-tall, quick enough, higher bottom bracket than gravel, enough clearance for mud, tires shaped by UCI race limits.
